# How much oat milk do you actually need this weekend?

A twenty-second paste answered it faster than a spreadsheet ever could.

---

Every small food business runs into the same Friday-afternoon guess: *how much do I prep for the weekend?*

Too little, and you're turning away customers by 11am on Saturday. Too much, and Sunday night ends with milk down the drain and dough in the trash. Most operators solve this the same way — squint at last weekend, add a little buffer, hope for the best.

Here's what that guess looks like with an actual trend line under it instead.

## The setup

Say you run a coffee cart, and Wednesday through Sunday of the past three weeks looked like this — cups sold, one number per day:

```
142
168
310
295
150
175
325
340
160
190
355
```

Eleven numbers. No spreadsheet, no dashboard — just what's in the till report or the register export.

## The paste

Drop that list into [Bearing](https://atruebearing.com), hit **Forecast forward**, and in under a second you get three things back:

**A number, not a vibe.** Next Saturday's forecast: roughly 370 cups, with a range of 310–430. Not "probably busy" — an actual band built from how much *this cart's own numbers* have swung week to week.

**The direction, at a glance.** The bearing strip shows the needle sitting solidly on "Rising" — about +9% versus the recent average. Three weeks of gentle upward drift, weekend spikes getting a little bigger each time.

**A next step, not just a chart.** *"Demand looks like it's trending up about 9%. Worth ordering or staffing a bit ahead of that curve rather than reacting after the fact."* That's the difference between a dashboard and a decision.

## Why the range matters as much as the number

A cart owner who's been eyeballing this by hand might round Saturday up to "around 350" and call it done. Bearing's answer isn't a cleaner-looking version of that same guess — it's the guess plus an honest admission of how much it could reasonably be off by. 310 on the low end still means over-ordering is unlikely to be a disaster. 430 on the high end is the number worth having oat milk in reserve for.

That range comes from the cart's *own* volatility — not an industry benchmark, not someone else's business. Three weeks of consistent weekend spikes produce a tighter range than three weeks of erratic ones would. The tool is only as confident as the data earns.

## What it doesn't know

Worth saying plainly, because Bearing says it plainly too: this is a straight-line read on recent history. It has no idea there's a street festival two blocks away next Saturday, or that the cart is trying a new seasonal drink. A trend line can't see the future — it can only tell you, honestly, where the recent past is pointing. That's still a better starting point than a gut-check with no data behind it, but it's a starting point, not a verdict.
